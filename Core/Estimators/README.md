# Core/Estimators

This directory contains implementations of estimators for information-theoretic quantities.

These estimators are used in `on_Model/` to compute quantities such as:
- Entropy (H)
- Mutual Information (MI)
- Transfer Entropy (TE)
- Reversed Transfer Entropy (rTE)

from time-series data generated by dynamical models.

---

## Conceptual Role

The estimators provide the **bridge between data and information dynamics**.

While the theoretical framework defines how information variables evolve,
these estimators allow those variables to be **measured from observed data**.

---

## Supported Quantities

The estimators are designed to compute:

- H(X)
- MI(X, Y)
- TE(X → Y)
- rTE (reverse transfer entropy)

These are computed from sampled data points constructed from time series.

---

## Estimation Methods

Two main approaches are considered in this framework:

### 1. Simple Binning (Histogram-Based Estimation)

This method:
- discretizes continuous variables into bins
- estimates probability distributions via counts
- computes entropy and related quantities from frequencies

Procedure:
1. Divide the state space into bins
2. Count occurrences in each bin
3. Estimate probabilities
4. Compute entropy using:

   H = - Σ p log p

Advantages:
- simple and fast
- easy to interpret
- useful for validation and debugging

Limitations:
- sensitive to bin size
- suffers from bias in high dimensions
- requires large data for accurate estimation

---

### 2. KSG Estimator (k-Nearest Neighbor)

The framework primarily relies on **KSG (Kraskov–Stögbauer–Grassberger)** estimation.

This method:
- avoids explicit binning
- uses distances to nearest neighbors in joint space
- estimates entropy via local density

Procedure:
1. Construct joint embedding vectors
2. Find k-nearest neighbors (typically using Chebyshev metric)
3. Estimate local densities
4. Compute entropy and mutual information

Advantages:
- well-suited for continuous variables
- lower bias compared to binning
- adapts to data distribution

Limitations:
- computationally heavier
- sensitive to choice of k
- variance can be high for small samples

---

## Workflow

Typical estimation pipeline:

1. Construct samples:
   - (X_t, Y_t, X_{t+1}, ...)
2. Define conditioning variables (for TE and rTE)
3. Apply estimator (binning or KSG)
4. Combine entropy terms to compute MI, TE, rTE

## Important Considerations

### Bias and Variance
- Binning: high bias, low variance (depends on bins)
- KSG: lower bias, higher variance for small samples

---

### Sample Size
- Reliable estimation requires sufficient data
- High-dimensional conditioning increases data demand

---

### Negative Estimates
- KSG and other nonparametric methods may produce small negative values
- These are artifacts of finite sampling

---

### Conditioning Structure
- TE and rTE require careful construction of conditioning sets
- Incorrect conditioning leads to misleading results

---

## Notes

- Both methods can be useful:
  - binning for quick checks
  - KSG for accurate estimation
- The framework is designed to allow comparison between estimators
